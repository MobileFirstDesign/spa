that are labeled &#8220;no fat&#8221;, &#8220;reduced in fat&#8221; &#8220;fat free&#8221; or &#8220;light&#8221;. The people who control labeling legislation have realized it is all very confusing to the average consumer. This is especially true when you realize that a product can contain fat and legally have &#8220;no fat&#8221; on its label.</p> <p>Milk producers, like many other sectors of the food industry felt the pressure of consumers looking to reduce the fat in their diets and as a result we now have a choice of whole milk, 2%, 1% and skim milk. But what can you write on the package of 2% milk. It obviously is lower in fat than whole milk, but is it low in fat? The United States Food and Drug Administration says no. They have a standard and 2% milk is above that standard. So 2% milk may carry the claim &quot;reduced in fat&quot; because it contains more than 25% less fat than whole milk. But 2% milk is not &quot;low fat&quot;. 1% milk qualifies as &quot;low fat&quot;.</p><p>Perhaps even more confusing is the fact that skim milk can carry the term &quot;nonfat&quot; or &quot;fat-free&quot; even though it does contain some fat. Again, the US Food and Drug Administration has a standard and when a food contains less than 0.5 grams of total fat per serving it is considered to contain no fat.
