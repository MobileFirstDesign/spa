But very often it just isn&#8217;t that way, especially in nature. We all know we need vitamins in our diets. For our convenience, scientists have given vitamins easy names to remember: vitamin A, vitamin C, vitamin D, vitamin E. But it&#8217;s a bit more complicated</p> <p>For example, there are several closely related chemical compounds that belong to the vitamin E family. Vitamin E has many specific uses in the body and, because the members of the vitamin E family have similar chemical structures, each one can be used in the body - but not with the same potency or bio-activity. Also, because of their different structures, the different vitamin Es are absorbed at different rates. &alpha;-tocopherol in the form of vitamin E is most common in supplements, while &gamma;-tocopherol is the form most common in the diet.</p><p>Current research indicates that, in spite of the differences in chemical structure, different members of the vitamin E family may be effective in preventing prostate cancer. In a large Finnish study it was reported that men with high blood levels of both &alpha;-tocopherol and &gamma;-tocopherol had a reduced risk of getting prostate cancer. The men in the study had received 50 mg &alpha;-tocopherol per day and 20 mg &beta;-carotene per day for 5-8 years and were compared to men receiving no supplements.
