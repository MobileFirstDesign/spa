class="dropcap">When it comes to listing nutrients that are important for good health and metabolism, vitamin K is not often at the top of most lists. But as the population ages, and bone health becomes more of a concern to many people, foods that are good sources of vitamin K may become more important in our diets.</p> <p>The Vitamin K story is a bit more complicated than other vitamins. To start off, there are in fact two forms of vitamin K. Vitamin K<sub>1</sub> is also known as phylloquinone, phytomenadione, or phytonadione. Vitamin K<sub>1</sub> is a family of chemically related compounds that differ in the length of their side chains. Vitamin K<sub>1</sub> is found in plant sources and Vitamin K<sub>2</sub> is found in animal sources. There are bacteria in our intestines that convert vitamin K<sub>1</sub> to vitamin K<sub>2</sub> which is the biologically active form for humans. The vitamin K found in food, combined that produced by our intestinal bacteria, play an important role in our metabolism. </p><p>Vitamin K is important in the building of bones. Vitamin K prevents the over-activation of osteoblast cell; this helps regulate the breakdown of bone.</p><p>Anyone who has compromised intestinal absorption due to such conditions as Crohn's disease or Celiac disease may be at risk of being Vitamin K deficient. Drugs such as colestyramine, salicylates, rifampin, isoniazid and barbiturates have been shown to reduce Vitamin K absorption.
